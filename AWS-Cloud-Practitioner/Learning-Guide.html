What is Cloud Computing?
The foundation — understanding the "why" before anything else

Before diving into AWS services and exam domains, you need to understand one thing clearly: what problem does cloud computing actually solve?

The Old Way vs The Cloud Way

Imagine you want to launch a website for your business. In the old world (before cloud), here is what you had to do:

Buy physical servers
Spend R500,000–R2,000,000 upfront on hardware before your business even earns a single rand. You have to guess how many users you'll have.
Find a place to put them
Rent a data center or build a server room — needs cooling, power backup, physical security, fire suppression.
Wait weeks for delivery and setup
Hardware must be ordered, shipped, racked, wired, and configured. This takes weeks, sometimes months.
Hire a full IT team
Network engineers, system admins, security specialists — just to keep the lights on. Not to build your product.
Overpay forever
Servers run 24/7 whether anyone uses them or not. You paid for peak capacity but most of the time it sits at 20% utilisation.
Real-World Analogy — Electricity

You don't build your own power station to get electricity for your house. You don't buy generators, turbines, and fuel storage. You just plug into the wall and pay Eskom for what you use.

Cloud computing is exactly the same for technology. Amazon built the power station (data centers, servers, networking). You plug in via the internet and pay only for what you use.

What AWS Actually Is

Amazon Web Services (AWS) is the world's largest cloud provider. They own more than 100 data centers globally, filled with millions of servers, petabytes of storage, and high-speed networking. They rent all of this to you on-demand, by the second, via the internet.

The 6 Benefits of the AWS Cloud

AWS officially describes six core benefits of moving to the cloud. These will appear directly on the exam — you need to know all six and understand each one.

#BenefitWhat it means in plain EnglishReal-world example
1 Trade fixed for variable expense Pay only for what you use, not upfront for hardware you might not need Instead of buying R1M of servers for a startup, pay R500/month while you're small
2 Benefit from massive economies of scale AWS buys millions of servers — cheaper per unit — and passes savings to you Buying one apple = R5. Buying a million apples wholesale = R0.30 each. AWS does this with hardware
3 Stop guessing capacity Scale up when busy, scale down when quiet — no more over-buying or under-buying A retail site scales up for Black Friday, back down in January — no wasted hardware
4 Increase speed and agility Launch new environments in minutes, not weeks. Experiment faster, fail cheaper A developer can spin up a test environment in 5 minutes instead of waiting 3 weeks for hardware
5 Stop spending on data centers AWS handles the physical infrastructure — you focus on your business, not server rooms No more IT staff just to keep servers running. That budget goes to building your product
6 Go global in minutes Deploy your app to multiple countries around the world with a few clicks A South African startup can serve Japanese users from an AWS server in Tokyo in under an hour
Exam Tip — Benefit #5 is often forgotten

Most people remember benefits 1, 3, 4, 6 easily. Don't forget "stop spending on data centers" — this is about freeing up operational focus, not just cost savings. And "go global in minutes" is specifically about deploying to multiple AWS Regions for geographic reach.

Cloud Service Models — IaaS, PaaS, SaaS

Cloud services come in three "layers." Think of building a house — you can buy raw land and build everything, buy a shell and finish it yourself, or buy a fully furnished apartment.

PaaS Platform as a Service Hardware + OS + runtime Your apps and data only AWS Elastic Beanstalk
SaaS Software as a Service Everything Just use it Amazon WorkMail, Gmail

Cloud Deployment Models

  • Most companies moving here
  • Example: Netflix on AWS

  • Your own cloud infrastructure
  • More control, more expensive
  • Example: A bank's internal servers

ℹ️

The AWS Well-Architected Framework — 6 Pillars

The Well-Architected Framework is AWS's guide to building good cloud systems. It has 6 pillars — think of them as 6 areas you always need to think about when designing anything on AWS.

Pillar 1
Operational Excellence
Run your systems well and keep improving. Automate tasks, respond to events, learn from failures, make small reversible changes.
Pillar 2
Security
Protect data, systems, and assets. Enable traceability, apply security everywhere, protect data in transit and at rest.
Pillar 3
Reliability
Recover automatically from failures, scale to meet demand, avoid single points of failure. Your app keeps working even when something breaks.
Pillar 4
Performance Efficiency
Use the right resource for the right job. Select the optimal instance types, use serverless where possible, monitor performance over time.
Pillar 5
Cost Optimization
Eliminate waste. Only pay for what you use. Right-size instances, use Reserved capacity for predictable workloads, delete unused resources.
Pillar 6
Sustainability
Minimize environmental impact. Use resources efficiently, choose energy-efficient services, scale to eliminate idle capacity.
Exam Tip — Know ALL 6 Pillars

The exam asks you to identify which pillar a scenario relates to. Security and Cost Optimization are the most tested. Don't forget Sustainability — it was added in 2021 and many people miss it.

Migration Strategies — The 7 Rs

When a company moves from on-premises to the cloud, they have seven different strategies. Think of this as a spectrum from "do nothing" to "rebuild everything."

StrategyPlain EnglishEffortExample
RetireShut it down — you don't need it anymoreNoneOld internal tool nobody uses
RetainKeep it on-premises for now, don't migrate yetNoneSystems with complex dependencies not ready to move
Rehost"Lift and shift" — move exactly as-is to AWS VMsLowCopy your web server to EC2 with no changes
RelocateMove at the hypervisor level (VMware on AWS)LowVMware environments moved to VMware Cloud on AWS
RepurchaseReplace with a SaaS product entirelyMediumReplace custom CRM with Salesforce
ReplatformMinor optimizations — keep the app, improve the platformMediumMove from self-managed MySQL to Amazon RDS (managed)
Refactor/Re-architectRedesign the app using cloud-native architectureHighRewrite monolith as microservices using Lambda + API Gateway
Memory Trick for the 7 Rs

Think of it as a spectrum of effort and reward: Retire/Retain = zero change. Rehost/Relocate = copy-paste. Repurchase = buy something new. Replatform = small improvements. Refactor = full rebuild.

The more work on the right side, the more you benefit from cloud-native features — but it costs more to get there.

Cloud Economics — Why Cloud Saves Money

Traditional IT (CapEx)
  • Large upfront hardware purchase (capital expenditure)
  • Servers sit idle most of the time (20–30% utilisation)
  • You buy for peak load, pay for it all year
  • Hardware depreciates over 3–5 years
  • You pay maintenance staff, electricity, cooling
AWS Cloud (OpEx)
  • No upfront cost — operational expenditure only
  • Pay exactly for what you use, when you use it
  • Scale up at peak, scale down overnight
  • AWS handles hardware replacement
  • AWS handles electricity, cooling, physical security

Rightsizing

Rightsizing means matching the size of your cloud resources to what you actually need — not over-provisioning. If your database only uses 10% of CPU on a large instance, you're wasting 90% of the money. Rightsizing = downgrade to a smaller instance that fits actual usage.

AWS provides AWS Compute Optimizer to recommend rightsizing based on your actual usage metrics.

BYOL vs Included Licenses

BYOL (Bring Your Own License): You already have a Windows or SQL Server license — you bring it to AWS and don't pay for it again.

Included License: You pay for the license as part of the AWS instance cost — no separate license needed.

The Shared Responsibility Model

This is the most important concept in the entire exam. It defines exactly what AWS is responsible for and what you (the customer) are responsible for.

The phrase AWS uses: "AWS is responsible for security OF the cloud. You are responsible for security IN the cloud."

The Apartment Building Analogy

AWS = the building management company. They are responsible for the building's structure, roof, lifts, electrical wiring, security guards at the entrance, CCTV in corridors, and the parking lot. You can't break into another tenant's apartment because building security is tight.

You = the tenant. You are responsible for locking your own apartment door, not giving your keys to strangers, securing your valuables, and not inviting bad people into your unit. The building management cannot control what you do inside your own apartment.

Key insight: AWS secures the building. But if you leave your door open — that's on you.

AWS is responsible for (Security OF the cloud)
  • Physical data center security
  • Server hardware and networking
  • Hypervisor and virtualization layer
  • Patching managed service OS (e.g. RDS patches its own OS)
  • Global network infrastructure
  • Storage hardware and deletion
You are responsible for (Security IN the cloud)
  • Your data and what you put in the cloud
  • IAM users, roles, and permissions
  • Patching EC2 operating systems and apps
  • Configuring security groups and network ACLs
  • Encrypting data at rest and in transit
  • Application-level security

How Responsibility Shifts by Service Type

⚠️
The key exam pattern for shared responsibility As you move from IaaS (EC2) → PaaS (Beanstalk) → managed services (RDS, Lambda), AWS takes on MORE responsibility. The more "managed" a service is, the more AWS handles and the less you need to worry about infrastructure security.

AWS Identity and Access Management (IAM)

IAM is how you control who can do what in your AWS account. Every action in AWS — launching a server, reading a file, sending an email — requires permission. IAM defines those permissions.

The Four Core IAM Concepts

👤
IAM User
A person (or application) with a username and password. Has permanent credentials. Example: you as a developer logging in to manage AWS resources.
Use when: A specific person needs long-term AWS access
👥
IAM Group
A collection of users. Attach a permission policy to the group once — all users in that group inherit those permissions. Much easier than managing individual users.
Use when: Multiple people need the same permissions (e.g. "Developers" group)
🎭
IAM Role
A temporary identity assumed by services (EC2, Lambda) or users from other accounts. Credentials expire automatically — much more secure than static keys.
Use when: An AWS service needs to call other AWS services, or for temporary access
📄
IAM Policy
A JSON document that says what actions are allowed or denied on which resources. Policies are attached to users, groups, or roles. Without a policy, nothing is allowed.
Use when: Defining exactly what permissions any identity has

The Root User — Handle with Extreme Care

When you create an AWS account, you create a root user — it has unlimited access to everything. Think of it as a nuclear code. AWS's best practice:

  • Enable MFA on root immediately
  • Create an IAM admin user for daily tasks
  • Never use root for day-to-day work
  • Only use root for specific tasks that require it (closing account, changing support plan)

Principle of Least Privilege

Simple Explanation

Give people only the minimum access they need to do their job — nothing more. If a developer only needs to read files from S3 and write to a DynamoDB table, give them exactly that. Not admin access to everything.

Why? If their account is ever compromised, the attacker can only do what that user is allowed to do. Limit the blast radius of any breach.

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

MFA requires two things to log in: something you know (password) + something you have (a code from your phone). Even if someone steals your password, they can't log in without the second factor.

Exam Tip — CloudTrail vs CloudWatch

CloudTrail = who did what, when (API audit log). CloudWatch = how is the resource performing (metrics, logs, alarms). These two are confused constantly. Ask yourself: "Is this about accountability (CloudTrail) or performance/health (CloudWatch)?"

AWS Organizations and Compliance

AWS Organizations lets you manage multiple AWS accounts centrally under one "management account." Key features:

  • Consolidated billing — one bill for all accounts, shared volume discounts
  • Service Control Policies (SCPs) — guardrails that limit what accounts can do, even if an admin sets permissions internally
  • Organizational Units (OUs) — group accounts (e.g. Production, Development) and apply different policies to each

Global Infrastructure — How AWS Is Organised Globally

AWS doesn't just have one big server farm. It has a globally distributed network of facilities, organised in a hierarchy.

TermWhat it isWhy it matters
Region A geographic area (e.g. Cape Town, US-East, Tokyo) with multiple data centers Data stays within the Region by default — important for data sovereignty laws
Availability Zone (AZ) One or more physically separate data centers within a Region, each with independent power, cooling, and networking Deploy across 2+ AZs for high availability — if one AZ fails, others keep running
Edge Location CloudFront CDN cache points in 400+ cities worldwide — not full data centers Cache content close to users for faster delivery. You can't run EC2 here
Local Zone AWS infrastructure placed in major metro areas for ultra-low latency compute For workloads that need single-digit millisecond latency to a specific city
AWS Outposts AWS rack of hardware installed physically in YOUR data center Run AWS services on-premises with the same APIs — for latency or data residency requirements
💡
High Availability vs Disaster Recovery Deploying across multiple AZs in one Region = high availability (survives a data center failure). Deploying across multiple Regions = disaster recovery (survives a regional disaster like a flood or power outage affecting an entire city).

Exam Tip — Security Group vs NACL

Security Group = stateful (return traffic automatically allowed), at the instance level. NACL = stateless (must manually allow both inbound AND outbound), at the subnet level. This distinction appears in exam questions regularly.

Application Integration Services

These services help different parts of your application talk to each other without being directly connected — this is called "decoupling."

Amazon SQS — Simple Queue Service
  • A message queue — one producer, one consumer
  • Consumer POLLS the queue for messages
  • Messages wait in the queue until consumed
  • Decouples services so they don't need to be available simultaneously
  • Think of it as: A text message inbox — you check it when you're ready
Amazon SNS — Simple Notification Service
  • Pub/Sub — one publisher, MANY subscribers
  • SNS PUSHES messages to all subscribers at once
  • Subscribers can be email, SMS, Lambda, SQS queues
  • Fan-out: one event goes to multiple consumers simultaneously
  • Think of it as: A group broadcast — everyone gets the message instantly

Amazon EventBridge routes events between AWS services based on rules. Think of it as a smart switchboard — "when an order is placed, send it to the fulfillment service AND the analytics service AND the email service."

AWS Step Functions orchestrates multi-step workflows. Chain Lambda functions, ECS tasks, and other services into a visual state machine with retries and error handling.

AWS Budgets Ongoing — set alerts on spending thresholds Define a monthly budget. Get email/SNS alert when you approach or exceed it
AWS Cost and Usage Report (CUR) Deep analysis — most detailed billing data Every line item of cost including resource IDs, tags, usage types. Exports to S3
AWS Compute Optimizer Rightsizing — reduce waste Analyses EC2 usage and recommends smaller/cheaper instances based on actual utilisation
Cost Allocation Tags Tracking which team/project spends what Tag resources with Team/Project/Environment. Filter billing reports by tag

Consolidated Billing in AWS Organizations

When you have multiple AWS accounts in an Organization, consolidated billing combines all usage into one bill. This has a powerful benefit: volume discounts are shared. If one account uses 5TB of S3 and another uses 5TB, together they look like 10TB — qualifying for a better pricing tier. Savings are passed back proportionally to each account.

Service Pattern: "Amazon" vs "AWS"

💡
Naming pattern that helps you recognise services Amazon = consumer-facing or foundational services (Amazon EC2, Amazon S3, Amazon RDS, Amazon DynamoDB)
AWS = management, developer, or platform tools (AWS IAM, AWS CloudFormation, AWS CodePipeline, AWS KMS)
Not a hard rule, but it helps when you encounter an unfamiliar service name on the exam.
